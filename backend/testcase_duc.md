  Ok so what have we actually been doing. Week 6 we established foundational plate tectonic theory — students can now describe the three main types of
  lithospheric plate boundaries (divergent, where plates move apart and new oceanic crust is generated through seafloor spreading at mid-ocean ridges;

  Week 7 — this week — we went hard on GEOMORPHOLOGICAL PROCESSES at coastal margins, specifically the intersection of lithospheric dynamics and coastal
  geomorphology. I want to walk you through this in some detail because it's complex and your child may need to talk it through at home. Coastal landforms are
  shaped by the dynamic interplay of: (a) marine erosional processes — hydraulic action (the sheer mechanical force of waves compressing air into rock fissures
  and exploding them apart), abrasion/corrasion (wave-transported sediment acting as sandpaper against the cliff face), attrition (eroded particles colliding
  with each other and becoming progressively smaller and more rounded), and solution/corrosion (chemical weathering by slightly acidic seawater on calcareous
  rock types like limestone); (b) marine depositional processes — longshore drift (the net transport of sediment along a coastline driven by the angle of wave
  approach), spit and bar formation, beach accretion; and (c) in tectonically active coastal settings, the additional variable of isostatic and eustatic sea
  level change. Students find the erosional landform sequence particularly engaging — we traced the development from a simple vertical cliff face through
  wave-cut notch → wave-cut platform → cave → arch → stack → stump, and connected each stage to a dominant erosional process. The Twelve Apostles on the
  Victorian coast are a perfect local example of stacks in various stages of this sequence.

  Now here is where it gets conceptually demanding — and this connects to the fieldwork at Hallett Cove — in tectonically active coastal zones (and southern

  neighbours, which itself is a comparative geography point worth making) the relationship between wave energy and coastal morphology is complicated by the
  possibility of sudden ground displacement during seismic events. the phenomenon of SOIL LIQUEFACTION — which occurs when saturated, loosely-packed granular
  sediment is subjected to the shear stress waves propagating outward from a seismic hypocentre, causing the sediment to temporarily lose its structural
  integrity and behave as a viscous fluid rather than a solid — is one of the most visually dramatic and practically destructive geological hazards associated
  with coastal and near-coastal environments. it is not caused by the wave shaking things down the way you might imagine — it is caused by the cyclic pore water
  pressure increase in the sediment matrix exceeding the effective confining stress. students found the video evidence from Christchurch 2011 absolutely
  gobsmacking and several of them spent a lunch break looking up liquefaction footage, which is exactly the kind of self-directed inquiry i want to see.

  BIOLOGY — UNIT 3: CONTINUITY OF LIFE — specifically Concept 3.2 onwards

  I want to spend some time this update laying out the conceptual architecture of where we are in the unit because we have now moved into territory that is
  genuinely philosophically interesting and i think students who understand the "big picture" of why we study these mechanisms will engage more deeply than
  students who are just memorising definitions for the exam.

  We have now completed our treatment of Mendelian genetics, meiotic recombination, and the chromosomal theory of inheritance. Students can (and will be assessed
   on their ability to) construct and interpret dihybrid crosses, apply chi-square goodness-of-fit tests to determine whether observed phenotypic ratios deviate
  significantly from predicted Mendelian ratios, and explain the significance of linked genes and crossing over as mechanisms that generate genetic variation in
  gametes. All of that is foundational and relatively settled science.

  Where we are going NOW is more interesting and more contested. We are entering the conceptual territory of phenotypic plasticity and epigenetic regulation —
  and i want to be precise about what these terms mean because they are often confused, even in popular science writing, and imprecision here will cost students
  marks. Phenotypic plasticity refers to the capacity of a single genotype (a specific DNA sequence) to produce a range of different phenotypic outcomes
  (observable characteristics — morphological, physiological, behavioural) in response to variation in environmental conditions. The key insight is that genotype
   does not deterministically specify phenotype — the expressed phenotype emerges from the interaction of genotype and environment, and the degree to which the
  phenotype can vary without changing the underlying DNA is the organism's phenotypic plasticity. A classic example: the water flea Daphnia, a single clonal
  genotype of which can develop either with or without a defensive "helmet" structure depending on whether chemical cues from predators are present in the water.
   Same DNA. Different body. Different survival strategy. This is phenotypic plasticity. Importantly, phenotypic plasticity is itself a heritable, selectable
  trait — populations under highly variable environmental pressures tend to evolve greater plasticity as an adaptive strategy.

  Epigenetic regulation is both related to and distinct from phenotypic plasticity. Where phenotypic plasticity describes the range of possible phenotypic
  outcomes, epigenetics refers to the specific molecular mechanisms by which gene expression is regulated WITHOUT altering the underlying nucleotide sequence of
  the DNA. These mechanisms include: DNA methylation (the addition of methyl groups — CH₃ — to cytosine residues, particularly at CpG dinucleotide sites,
  typically via the enzyme DNA methyltransferase — DNMT — resulting in transcriptional silencing of the associated gene by restricting transcription factor
  access or recruiting repressor complexes); histone modification (post-translational chemical modifications — acetylation, methylation, phosphorylation,
  ubiquitination — to the histone proteins around which DNA is wound, altering chromatin compaction and therefore the accessibility of the DNA to the
  transcriptional machinery); and non-coding RNA regulation (particularly microRNAs and long non-coding RNAs that post-transcriptionally regulate mRNA stability
  and translation). The critical — and for ATAR students, assessable — point is that some epigenetic marks are heritable across cell divisions (mitotically
  heritable) and there is growing, though still contested, evidence that some marks may be transgenerationally heritable across generations via the germline
  (meiotic inheritance of epigenetic state). This is a genuinely active research frontier and i will ask exam questions that specifically test students' ability
  to distinguish between established consensus science and emerging/contested findings.

  The conceptual bridge i am building in class — and this will become explicit in Week 7 — is the connection between epigenetic gene regulation, phenotypic
  plasticity, and the agricultural and environmental applications these mechanisms open up. Specifically: if we can understand the molecular switches that
  control which genes are expressed under what environmental conditions, can we deliberately manipulate those switches to produce crop plants with enhanced
  yield, drought tolerance, or pest resistance WITHOUT permanently altering the genome (which triggers a different regulatory and commercial pathway than
  conventional GM crops)? This is the question driving significant commercial R&D investment globally and it is where the Economics integration becomes
  non-trivial.

  ECONOMICS — UNIT 3: THE AUSTRALIAN AND GLOBAL ECONOMY — Resource Allocation Under Constraint

  In parallel with the biology content, in Economics we have been developing the conceptual toolkit for analysing macroeconomic resource allocation decisions
  under conditions of scarcity and uncertainty. Students should at this stage be fluent with: the production possibilities frontier (PPF) as a model of
  opportunity cost at a societal level — the idea that in a world of finite productive resources, the decision to allocate more factors of production (land,
  labour, capital, enterprise) toward one output category necessarily means forgoing potential output in another; comparative advantage and specialisation as the
   theoretical basis for both domestic industrial policy and international trade patterns; and the role of market failure — particularly in the context of
  negative externalities, public goods, and information asymmetry — in justifying government intervention through regulation, taxation (Pigouvian instruments),
  subsidies, or direct provision.

  Where this connects to the biology content — and this is the cross-curricular integration we are explicitly building this term — is in the economics of
  biotechnology and agricultural innovation under climate stress. Consider the following scenario, which we will use as a case study in Week 6: a nation with
  comparative advantage in wheat production faces a projected 15–20% reduction in arable land productivity over the next 30 years due to increasing aridity
  driven by climate change. The government must allocate a constrained R&D budget across competing interventions: conventional plant breeding programs (long time
   horizon, lower unit cost, no regulatory friction), transgenic GM crop development (medium time horizon, high regulatory and commercial costs, significant
  public acceptance barriers, potentially high yield payoff), and epigenetic/RNA-interference-based agronomic interventions (shorter regulatory pathway than full
   GMO in some jurisdictions, medium time horizon, novel IP landscape, potentially reversible effects). from a macroeconomic resource allocation perspective this
   is a multi-period, multi-variable optimisation problem under genuine uncertainty, and the opportunity cost of choosing any one pathway is the foregone benefit
   of the alternative. what makes this an interesting economics problem rather than just a science policy question is that the decision is made not in a vacuum
  but within institutional contexts — property rights regimes, international trade agreements (particularly the IP provisions of bilateral FTAs), consumer
  preference structures, and the political economy of agricultural subsidies — that strongly shape which option is economically rational even if it is not
  scientifically optimal.

  Students will write an integrated essay in Week 8 addressing the following question: "Evaluate the claim that epigenetic approaches to crop modification
  represent a more economically rational allocation of agricultural R&D resources than transgenic GMO development under conditions of climate-driven resource
  constraint in the Australian context." This requires students to deploy biological understanding of the mechanisms involved (you cannot evaluate the claim
  without understanding what epigenetic modification actually does and how it differs from transgenesis), economic analytical frameworks (PPF, opportunity cost,
  market failure, externalities), and critical evaluation skills (distinguishing between empirical claims, normative claims, and claims that blend both). it is,
  deliberately, a hard question. i am not apologising for that.
